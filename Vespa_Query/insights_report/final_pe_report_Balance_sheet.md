*   **Working Capital Intensity:** Board remuneration is a fixed operating expense and does not directly exacerbate Working Capital Intensity (WCI). These tax benefits are typically recognized as **deferred tax assets**. When an entity incurs a loss, it often generates tax loss carryforwards. If it is probable that the entity will generate sufficient future taxable profits against which these losses can be utilized, a deferred tax asset is recognized on the balance sheet. The recognition of this asset results in a corresponding tax benefit being recorded on the income statement, which reduces the reported net loss for the period [General PE insight]. **Impact on True Free Cash Flow Conversion:**

The recognition of a deferred tax asset is a **non-cash accounting entry**. It improves the reported net income (by reducing the net loss) but does not represent a cash inflow from operations in the current period. However, the underlying reason for the tax benefit—operational losses—means that the entities likely paid little to no cash taxes in FY2023. This absence of a cash outflow for taxes would positively impact the operating cash flow, making it higher (or less negative) than if the entities were profitable and incurring a cash tax expense.

To assess **true Free Cash Flow Conversion**, it is crucial to distinguish between the accounting benefit and the actual cash flow impact:

*   **Accounting Benefit:** The tax benefit inflates the reported net income (reduces the net loss) without a corresponding cash inflow. This can make the reported profitability appear better than the underlying operational performance suggests.
*   **Cash Flow Impact:** The fact that no cash taxes were paid (or a minimal amount) due to the losses means that cash was conserved within the business. This conservation of cash *does* improve the Free Cash Flow relative to a scenario where the entities were profitable and paying taxes. However, this improvement is a consequence of operational underperformance leading to no tax liability, rather than strong operational cash generation. Therefore, while the tax benefit improves the reported net loss, the underlying operational underperformance indicates that the core business is not generating sufficient cash from its activities. Analysts should adjust for the non-cash nature of deferred tax movements when evaluating operational cash flow and FCF conversion to get a clearer picture of the entities' financial health.

**Q:** Debt increased by SAR 2,298,066 to SAR 30,265,745 in 2024, yet "Other non-cash movements" for debt remain significant at SAR 1,935,345. **Analysis**

The substantial "Other non-cash movements" for debt, amounting to SAR 1,935,345 in 2024, primarily refer to interest attributable to debt [1](https://s3-qa-8byte.s3.ap-south-1.amazonaws.com/sabic%2Fin/sabic-integrated-annual-report-2024-en_tcm1010-46870.pdf#page=216). The provided information does not offer a more granular forensic breakdown to clarify if these movements specifically represent capitalized interest, deferred debt issuance costs, or other detailed adjustments beyond this general classification of "interest attributable to debt." Therefore, the exact composition of these non-cash movements is not fully detailed in the available data.

**General PE Insight on Impact:**

*   **Obscuring True Cash Cost of Debt:** Since these movements are non-cash, they indicate that a portion of the interest expense is not paid in cash during the period. This could be due to accrued interest that has not yet been disbursed, or capitalized interest where interest costs are added to the principal balance of the debt rather than being expensed immediately as a cash outflow. This indeed obscures the true cash cost of debt, as the reported debt balance increases without a corresponding cash payment for that specific amount of interest.

*   **Impact on ROIC (Return on Invested Capital):** ROIC is typically calculated as NOPAT (Net Operating Profit After Tax) divided by Invested Capital. Non-cash interest movements can impact ROIC in a few ways:
    *   If the interest is accrued but not paid, it would typically be expensed in the income statement, reducing EBIT and thus NOPAT. However, if it's capitalized interest, it would not directly reduce EBIT in the period it's incurred but would increase the debt balance.
    *   The "Invested Capital" component of ROIC includes total debt. Any non-cash movement that increases the debt balance (such as capitalized interest or accrued interest that adds to the principal) will increase the denominator of the ROIC calculation, potentially leading to a lower ROIC, even without a cash investment.

*   **Impact on LTM Debt-to-EBITDA Bridge:** The LTM (Last Twelve Months) Debt-to-EBITDA ratio uses the total debt balance as its numerator. Non-cash movements, such as the SAR 1,935,345 in interest attributable to debt, directly increase the reported debt balance [1](https://s3-qa-8byte.s3.ap-south-1.amazonaws.com/sabic%2Fin/sabic-integrated-annual-report-2024-en_tcm1010-46870.pdf#page=216). While EBITDA (Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization) is unaffected by non-cash interest movements (as interest is excluded from EBITDA), the increase in the debt numerator will directly impact the LTM Debt-to-EBITDA ratio. This would show a higher leverage ratio, potentially without a corresponding cash inflow to the business or a cash outflow for the interest, thereby affecting the LTM Debt-to-EBITDA bridge by increasing the debt component.

### Related Party Debt: FCF/ROIC Justification

**Q:** The non-current portion of related party loans has surged by SAR 3.65 billion YoY, reaching SAR 7.40 billion, representing a disproportionate increase compared to the total debt growth of SAR 2.30 billion. Explain how this massive related party financing directly translates into improved Free Cash Flow Conversion and an elevated ROIC, rather than acting as a life support mechanism masking underlying operational deficiencies.


![Chart]( ./test_plots/s23_q1.png)

**Analysis**

The significant surge in the non-current portion of related party loans, reaching SAR 7.40 billion, can directly translate into improved Free Cash Flow (FCF) Conversion and an elevated Return on Invested Capital (ROIC) primarily due to the advantageous terms of this financing, rather than indicating underlying operational deficiencies. The key factors are:

1.  **Interest-Free Nature of Loans:** The most critical aspect is that outstanding balances with related parties, including loans, are explicitly stated to be **interest-free** [3]. This directly impacts both FCF Conversion and ROIC:
    *   **Improved FCF Conversion:** By eliminating interest expenses on a substantial portion of debt (SAR 7.40 billion), the company significantly reduces its cash outflows related to financing activities. This directly increases the cash available from operations and enhances the conversion of earnings into free cash flow, as less cash is diverted to service debt [3].
    *   **Elevated ROIC:** ROIC measures how efficiently a company generates profits from its invested capital. When a significant portion of the capital employed (debt) is interest-free, the effective cost of capital is substantially lowered. This allows the company to fund profitable projects and operations without the drag of interest expenses, thereby leading to a higher return on the invested capital [3].

2.  **Enhanced Liquidity and Financial Flexibility:** The increase in related party borrowing also includes a substantial increase in the three-year revolving credit facility (RCF) with Aramco Overseas Company, from SAR 3.75 billion to SAR 11.25 billion [1]. This expanded facility provides significant liquidity and financial flexibility, which can be used for general corporate purposes and refinancing maturing debt [1]. Access to such flexible and low-cost capital supports stable operations and strategic investments without straining operational cash flows, further contributing to FCF conversion.

3.  **Strategic Portfolio Optimization:** The company is actively engaged in divesting non-core assets, such as Hadeed and shares in Aluminium Bahrain B.S.C. (ALBA), to "optimise its portfolio, focus on its core business, and further strengthen its growth in the chemicals industry" [4]. While the related party financing is not explicitly linked to these specific divestments, the overall strategy of focusing on core, high-return businesses, supported by low-cost capital, ensures that invested capital (including that from related parties) is directed towards opportunities that are expected to generate higher returns, thus elevating ROIC.

**Addressing the 'Life Support' Concern:**
The interest-free nature of these substantial loans, coupled with the increase in committed credit facilities and a clear strategy for portfolio optimization, strongly suggests a deliberate financial advantage provided by a related party (e.g., a parent company like Saudi Aramco [7]) rather than a desperate measure to mask operational deficiencies. Companies in distress typically face higher borrowing costs and more restrictive terms, not interest-free, unsecured financing [3]. This indicates robust financial backing and a strategic approach to capital management.

Under a stress scenario of an additional 150 basis point (bps) hike, the pro forma impact on interest expense (and consequently, profit before tax) can be estimated by extrapolating from the provided 100 bps sensitivity data for December 31, 2024 [1](https://s3-qa-8byte.s3.ap-south-1.amazonaws.com/sabic%2Fin/sabic_in_sabic_cc_compressed.pdf#page=245):
