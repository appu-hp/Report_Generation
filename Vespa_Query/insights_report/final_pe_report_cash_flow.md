**Analysis**

Management, the 'Other non-cash movements' for dividends represent appropriations from retained earnings for both shareholders and non-controlling interests (NCIs) [1](https://s3-qa-8byte.s3.ap-south-1.amazonaws.com/sabic%2Fin/sabic-integrated-annual-report-2024-en_tcm1010-46870.pdf#page=216). These are essentially declared dividends that have not yet been paid out in cash, leading to an increase in 'Dividends payable' on the balance sheet [2](https://s3-qa-8byte.s3.ap-south-1.amazonaws.com/sabic%2Fin/sabic-integrated-annual-report-2024-en_tcm1010-46870.pdf#page=230). Here's how these recurring, significant non-cash entries impact Free Cash Flow (FCF) Conversion and the true economic capital available for LPs:

*   **Impact on Free Cash Flow Conversion:** While the *declaration* of a dividend (a non-cash movement) does not directly impact FCF, as FCF is typically calculated before financing activities like dividend payments, the *crystallization* of these declared dividends into future cash liabilities significantly affects FCF conversion. These declared but unpaid obligations mean that a substantial portion of future operating cash flow will be committed to settling these liabilities. For instance, in 2024, the company reported FCF of SAR 6.16 billion [4](https://s3-qa-8byte.s3.ap-south-1.amazonaws.com/sabic%2Fin/sabic_in_sabic_cc_compressed.pdf#page=63), while total dividends payable stood at SAR 7.47 billion [2](https://s3-qa-8byte.s3.ap-south-1.amazonaws.com/sabic%2Fin/sabic-integrated-annual-report-2024-en_tcm1010-46870.pdf#page=230). This indicates that a significant portion of the FCF is effectively pre-committed, reducing the discretionary cash available for other strategic uses. The actual cash outflows for dividends are recorded under financing activities [4](https://s3-qa-8byte.s3.ap-south-1.amazonaws.com/sabic%2Fin/sabic_in_sabic_cc_compressed.pdf#page=63).

*   **Impact on True Economic Capital Available for LPs:** These non-cash movements create a 'Dividends payable' liability [2](https://s3-qa-8byte.s3.ap-south-1.amazonaws.com/sabic%2Fin/sabic-integrated-annual-report-2024-en_tcm1010-46870.pdf#page=230), which is a direct claim on the company's future cash. Although the cash remains within the company until the dividend is paid, it is not truly 'available' for other uses or for discretionary distribution to LPs, as it is already committed. This reduces the unencumbered economic capital that could otherwise be deployed for strategic investments, growth initiatives, or additional distributions. The company's priority of distributing stable-to-growing, competitive dividends reinforces that these declared amounts are firm commitments that will crystallize into cash outflows [5](https://s3-qa-8byte.s3.ap-south-1.amazonaws.com/sabic%2Fin/sabic_in_sabic_cc_compressed.pdf#page=19).

**General PE insight:** From a Private Equity perspective, these declared but unpaid dividends represent a reduction in the company's financial flexibility and liquidity. While not an immediate cash drain, they are a known future obligation that must be factored into any assessment of the company's true cash-generating capacity and its ability to fund future growth or provide additional distributions beyond these declared amounts. It implies that the 'cash outflows' for dividends, which are part of financing activities [4](https://s3-qa-8byte.s3.ap-south-1.amazonaws.com/sabic%2Fin/sabic_in_sabic_cc_compressed.pdf#page=63), will continue to be substantial due to these prior declarations.
