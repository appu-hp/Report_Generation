### Granular ROIC: CapEx Nature?

![Chart](./test_plots_income/s4_q2.png)

**Analysis**

The granular LTM Return on Invested Capital (ROIC) for 2024 is **3.05%**.

**Calculation:**
1.  **Net Operating Profit After Tax (NOPAT):**
    *   Income from operations (EBIT) for 2024: SAR 5.74 Bn. [6](https://s3-qa-8byte.s3.ap-south-1.amazonaws.com/sabic%2Fin/sabic-integrated-annual-report-2024-en_tcm1010-46870.pdf#page=61)

    *   Effective Tax Rate = Income tax and Zakat / (EBIT + Income tax and Zakat) = 0.14 / (5.74 + 0.14) = 2.38%
    *   NOPAT = EBIT * (1 - Effective Tax Rate) = 5.74 Bn. * (1 - 0.0238) = SAR 5.60 Bn.

2.  **Invested Capital (as of December 31, 2024):**
    *   Total Equity: SAR 183.92 Bn. (Equity attributable to equity holders of the Parent SAR 156.83 Bn. + Non-controlling interests SAR 27.09 Bn.) [5](https://s3-qa-8byte.s3.ap-south-1.amazonaws.com/sabic%2Fin/sabic-integrated-annual-report-2024-en_tcm1010-46870.pdf#page=62)

    *   Invested Capital = Total Equity + Total Debt - Cash and Cash Equivalents = 183.92 + 30.27 - 30.54 = SAR 183.65 Bn.

3.  **ROIC = NOPAT / Invested Capital = 5.60 Bn. / 183.65 Bn. = 0.0305 or 3.05%.**

### JV Dividends: Losses, Capital, FCF Impact

**Q:** The "Results of integral joint ventures" show a P&L loss of SAR 765,158 in 2024 and SAR 931,459 in 2023, yet the company consistently reports SAR 1,787,474 in "Dividend received from integral joint ventures." Explain how these JVs are generating such substantial cash dividends while reporting significant P&L losses; is this a return of capital, and how does this distort our Free Cash Flow Conversion?


![Chart](./test_plots_income/s13_q1.png)

**Analysis**

The observation regarding a P&L loss from integral joint ventures (JVs) while substantial dividends are received requires clarification based on the financial statements provided. The "Results from integral joint ventures" actually show a *profit* of SAR 765,158 in 2024 and SAR 931,459 in 2023 in the Consolidated Statement of Income [3](https://s3-qa-8byte.s3.ap-south-1.amazonaws.com/sabic%2Fin%2Fsabic_in_sabic_cc_compressed.pdf#page=172). These positive figures represent the Group's share of the JVs' net income, as clarified in Note 10 [6](https://s3-qa-8byte.s3.ap-south-1.amazonaws.com/sabic%2Fin%2Fsabic-integrated-annual-report-2024-en_tcm1010-46870.pdf#page=202). The negative figures (SAR 765,158 in 2024 and SAR 931,459 in 2023) appearing in the Consolidated Statement of Cash Flows are an *adjustment* to reconcile income before zakat and income tax to net cash from operating activities [1](https://s3-qa-8byte.s3.ap-south-1.amazonaws.com/sabic%2Fin%2Fsabic_in_sabic_cc_compressed.pdf#page=175). This adjustment removes the non-cash equity method income from the JVs, as the actual cash flow from these JVs is represented by the dividends received. Therefore, the JVs are not reporting P&L losses from the parent company's perspective; rather, they are contributing to the parent's net income.The explanation for the substantial cash dividends while reporting lower P&L income from JVs is rooted in the difference between accrual accounting (equity method) and cash accounting:

*   **Equity Method Accounting:** The company recognizes its share of the integral joint ventures' net income or loss in its Consolidated Statement of Income [3](https://s3-qa-8byte.s3.ap-south-1.amazonaws.com/sabic%2Fin%2Fsabic_in_sabic_cc_compressed.pdf#page=172), which is an accrual-based entry and does not directly represent cash received.
*   **Cash Dividends:** The actual cash received from these JVs is reported as "Dividend received from integral joint ventures" in the Consolidated Statement of Cash Flows [1](https://s3-qa-8byte.s3.ap-south-1.amazonaws.com/sabic%2Fin%2Fsabic_in_sabic_cc_compressed.pdf#page=175). These dividends are distributions of the JVs' accumulated earnings, which may include profits from prior periods that were not distributed at the time they were earned.

**Is this a return of capital?**

Given that the dividends received (SAR 1,787,474 in both 2024 and 2023) [1](https://s3-qa-8byte.s3.ap-south-1.amazonaws.com/sabic%2Fin%2Fsabic_in_sabic_cc_compressed.pdf#page=175) significantly exceed the Group's share of the integral joint ventures' net income (SAR 765,158 in 2024 and SAR 931,459 in 2023) [3](https://s3-qa-8byte.s3.ap-south-1.amazonaws.com/sabic%2Fin%2Fsabic_in_sabic_cc_compressed.pdf#page=172), it is highly probable that a portion of these dividends represents a return of capital or a distribution of accumulated retained earnings from previous periods that were not distributed earlier. The financial statements do not explicitly label any portion of these dividends as a return of capital, but the cash flow dynamics suggest it.

**How does this distort Free Cash Flow Conversion?**

Free Cash Flow (FCF) conversion is typically assessed by comparing operating cash flow to a measure of earnings (e.g., Net Income, EBITDA). The inclusion of "Dividend received from integral joint ventures" within "Net cash from operating activities" [1](https://s3-qa-8byte.s3.ap-south-1.amazonaws.com/sabic%2Fin%2Fsabic_in_sabic_cc_compressed.pdf#page=175) can distort FCF conversion in the following ways:

*   **Inflation of Operating Cash Flow:** If a significant portion of the dividends received is a return of capital or a distribution of prior-period earnings, it inflates the current period's operating cash flow without a corresponding increase in the current period's reported net income from the JVs. This can make the company's cash generation appear stronger than its current operational profitability from these ventures.
*   **Misleading Conversion Ratio:** A higher operating cash flow, driven by these substantial dividends that exceed current earnings, could lead to an artificially high FCF conversion ratio (Operating Cash Flow / Net Income). This might suggest a more efficient conversion of profits into cash than is truly the case from ongoing operations.

**General PE insight:**

From a Private Equity perspective, it is crucial to analyze the sustainability and nature of these dividends. While the equity method reflects the economic interest, the actual cash distributions are what matter for liquidity and further investment. If dividends consistently outpace current period earnings, further due diligence would be warranted to understand the JVs' underlying cash generation, capital structure, and dividend policies. This would help ascertain whether the distributions are sustainable, funded by genuine operational cash flow, or if they are indicative of asset sales, increased leverage within the JVs, or simply a distribution of a large pool of historical retained earnings. For FCF analysis, it would be prudent to adjust the operating cash flow to reflect only the portion of dividends that corresponds to the current period's share of JV earnings, or to analyze the JVs' standalone FCF generation, to get a clearer picture of the parent company's core FCF conversion.

Without a more granular breakdown of the "Others" column for Murabaha and Bonds, it is not possible to explicitly confirm the precise reconciliation of these specific elements beyond the general statement provided for total debt.

**General PE insight:** Non-cash movements in debt can include items such as the amortization of debt discounts or premiums, fair value adjustments, foreign exchange differences on debt denominated in foreign currencies, or the capitalization of interest. Proper accounting and disclosure of these items as non-cash adjustments are crucial for transparent financial reporting and accurate FCF analysis.
